A UNIVERSE OF OPPORTUNITY
An analysis on jobs for DSAN students
The current job market is scary! Navigating job-searching and landing a job in time for graduation can feel like being lost in space. With endless listings and rejection emails, the process can be overwhelming. For international students, the gravity of the situation is even more pressing. Fortunately, the DSAN program taught us to make sense of things by analyzing data! This project's data comes from a web-crawl that identified job posts of interest to the DSAN community. 10 query terms were used (e.g. data scientist, data analyst, etc.) for locations both in D.C. and across the U.S. After removing duplicates values, we were left with 500+ jobs. This analysis explores the data to help students understand the job market and find what they need to be successful.
Which jobs are available? My first question was: which jobs are available and who is offering them? Fig. 1.1 shows the 30 most common job titles of our search; Data Scientist, Data Analyst, and Machine Learning Engineer are the most needed positions. The other two common titles involve NLP or Blockchain technology.

It makes sense for a DSAN search to yield such results. Fortunately, the DSAN program prepares us for all of these roles, so students should feel prepared to take any of them on. However, job titles are not always indicative of the actual work. For example, a Data Scientist could be doing more data engineering than data science. It is important to read job descriptions to know what will be expected of you.
Who is offering them?
Job-searchers often want to know which company is offering the position. After all, culture and values can be a deal-maker! Fig. 1.2 shows us the 30 most common companies offering jobs. Upwork, Booz Allen, Apple, Deloitte, and Walmart show up most frequently.

Quick research on Upwork revealed that its a freelancing site. The job posts were for contract work, which is not ideal for students looking for full-time positions. I would advise DSAN students to look past these postings and instead focus on more traditional, full-time jobs. The other common companies from the dataset are all well-known and have a presence in the D.C. area. This is great news for students who want to stay in the city after graduation!
Looking at schedule types Figure 2.2 shows the distribution of time commitments expected by the companies. It's reassuring to find that most of them are full-time, which is best for stability and consistency. However, it's interesting that the contracting jobs from Upwork take up close to 10% of the total count.
Can we work from home?
The data was a bit more ambiguous when it came to the work schedule. A work_from_home column had a T/F value, but many job descriptions or benefits also mentioned the possibility of working from home. It is possible that the ratio is a bit higher, but fig. 2.2 shows that about 27% of jobs are remote. I believe that the COVID cycle has ushered a new era of acceptance for work outside the office, so I would expect this number to increase in the future.
Let's talk about money! DSAN jobs are high-paying: the lower end starts around $100K and they tend to max out around $200K. However, there is a big range for each salary (~$60K). This is most likely due to experience amount and skills obtained prior to the job.

 How about benefits?
401K, health/life/dental insurance, bonus, tuition payments are all considered to be prime benefits. Fig 3.2 shows that 33% of the jobs that listed benefits made no mention of prime benefits, and most of those who did offered less than three of them. Salary and benefits are two of the most important factors when considering a job; I would recommend recruiters to clearly outline these benefits in their job descriptions to attract more candidates.
What are the responsibilities? Getting a count of each unique word from all the responsibilities text for each job allowed me to create the bubble plot. The bigger the bubble, the more common the word. With this graph available, we can take a look at what type of tasks we'll be expected to do in the job.

As expected of DSAN roles, the most common words are data, learning, experience, and many others that signal machine learning and data science. However, there are also words like business, team, and management. This shows that companies are looking for well-rounded candidates who can work with others and understand the business side of things; a skill set that our program has focused on. Our students should feel prepared to take on any of these roles.
Which skills do we need?
Usually the job responsibilities will list the skills required for a position, but the qualifications sections could offer a bit more detail on what the company is looking for. For this plot, I used the same method as the responsibilities plot.

There is some overlap in the word counts, but the qualifications sections list more hard skills such as Python and programming. Not surprisingly, teamwork and communication are also listed as important skills. I would've expected SQL and possibly other languages to be more common, but this shows Python to be the most popular program, and soft skills to be as important as hard skills.
Can we predict with the variables? With a clear understanding of the job market for DSAN students, I wanted to see if there were relationships between variables. I wondered if jobs with higher counts of responsibilities or qualifications would beget higher salaries. Figure 5.1 shows us the correlations between variables.

Unfortunately, the data doesn't show any strong correlations. The only columns with a correlation above 0.5 are the columns regarding salary, but the jobs themselves give us the range. At best, we'd be able to predict a higher range if they mention that salary is negotiable and offer an initial amount.
What did we learn? Many companies are in need of DSAN students and offer high salaries, some benefits, and relative work flexibility to lure in candidates. They also mainly prefer full-time employees.
Landing a job requires both hard and soft skills, with an emphasis on communication and teamwork.
Employers want us to have knowledge of Python, machine/deep learning, and data analysis.
Available variables offer no predictability of other variables. More data, and cleaner data, is needed to have a better chance at predicting variables.
The DSAN program has taught us a lot of the skills needed to succeed, but it's up to our out-of-school work, experiences, and learning to set us apart.
